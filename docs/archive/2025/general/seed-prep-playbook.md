# Seed Funding Prep Playbook

**Purpose:** Complete checklist and guide for preparing to raise seed funding, including pitch deck, investor outreach, and due diligence preparation.

## Overview

This playbook walks you through everything needed to successfully raise seed funding for your Canadian venture, from initial preparation through closing.

## Pre-Fundraising Checklist

### Company Foundation

- [ ] Company incorporated (federal or provincial)
- [ ] Business registration complete
- [ ] GST/HST registration (if applicable)
- [ ] Bank account opened
- [ ] Basic bookkeeping system in place
- [ ] Founders' agreement signed
- [ ] Intellectual property ownership clarified
- [ ] Domain names and trademarks registered

### Product & Market

- [ ] MVP launched and validated
- [ ] Traction metrics documented (users, revenue, growth)
- [ ] Product-market fit demonstrated
- [ ] Market size research completed
- [ ] Competitive analysis done
- [ ] Unique value proposition clear

### Financials

- [ ] Financial statements prepared (if applicable)
- [ ] Burn rate calculated
- [ ] Runway projection (months until out of cash)
- [ ] Funding amount needed determined
- [ ] Use of funds plan created
- [ ] Financial projections (12-24 months)
- [ ] Unit economics understood

### Team

- [ ] Core team assembled
- [ ] Roles and responsibilities defined
- [ ] Advisor relationships established
- [ ] Advisory board formed (optional)

## Pitch Deck Structure

## Investor Outreach Strategy

### Target Investor Types

**Angel Investors:**
- High-net-worth individuals
- Often founders themselves
- Typically invest $10K-100K CAD
- More hands-on, mentorship-focused

**Seed Funds:**
- Early-stage VC funds
- Typically invest $100K-1M CAD
- More structured process
- Portfolio approach

**Accelerators/Incubators:**
- Y Combinator, Techstars, etc.
- Provide funding + program
- Network and mentorship
- Demo day at end

**Family Offices:**
- Wealthy families' investment vehicles
- Can invest significant amounts
- Often patient capital

### Finding Investors

**Warm Introductions (Best):**
- Ask advisors, mentors, other founders
- LinkedIn connections
- Industry events and conferences
- Alumni networks

**Cold Outreach:**
- Research investors who invest in your space
- Personalize emails (reference portfolio companies)
- Use LinkedIn to find mutual connections
- Be respectful and concise

**Platforms:**
- AngelList (now Wellfound)
- Gust
- Crunchbase (to find investors)
- LinkedIn Sales Navigator

### Outreach Email Template

**Subject:** [Company Name] - [Tagline/Mission]

**Body:**

## Investor Meeting Prep

### Before the Meeting

- [ ] Research the investor/VC
- [ ] Review their portfolio companies
- [ ] Understand their investment thesis
- [ ] Prepare answers to common questions
- [ ] Practice your pitch
- [ ] Prepare demo (if applicable)

### Common Questions to Prepare For

**About the Problem:**
- Why is this problem worth solving?
- How big is the problem?
- What are people doing now?

**About the Solution:**
- How does your solution work?
- Why is your approach better?
- What's your competitive advantage?

**About the Market:**
- How big is the market?
- How fast is it growing?
- Who are your customers?

**About Traction:**
- What traction do you have?
- What are your key metrics?
- What's your growth rate?

**About the Team:**
- Why are you the right team?
- What relevant experience do you have?
- How will you scale the team?

**About the Business:**
- How do you make money?
- What are your unit economics?
- What's your path to profitability?

**About the Ask:**
- How much are you raising?
- What will you use the funds for?
- What milestones will this enable?

### During the Meeting

- Be authentic and passionate
- Listen to feedback
- Ask questions about their process
- Don't oversell
- Be honest about challenges
- Show that you're coachable

### After the Meeting

- Send thank-you email within 24 hours
- Answer any follow-up questions promptly
- Provide requested materials quickly
- Keep them updated on progress
- Follow up appropriately (don't pester)

## Term Sheet Basics

### Key Terms

**Valuation:**
- Pre-money valuation: Company value before investment
- Post-money valuation: Pre-money + investment amount

**Investment Amount:**
- How much you're raising
- Minimum/maximum raise amounts

**Equity:**
- Percentage of company being sold
- Example: $500K at $2M pre-money = 20% equity

**Vesting:**
- Founder shares typically vest over 4 years
- 1-year cliff, then monthly

**Board Seats:**
- How many board members
- Who gets seats

**Liquidation Preference:**
- Investor gets paid first in exit
- Typically 1x (their investment back)

**Anti-Dilution:**
- Protection against down rounds
- Weighted average or full ratchet

**Pro-Rata Rights:**
- Right to invest in future rounds
- Maintain ownership percentage

### Getting Help

- **Lawyers:** Essential for term sheet review
- **Accountants:** For financial structuring
- **Advisors:** For negotiation strategy
- **Other Founders:** For experience sharing

## Funding Alternatives

### Before Raising Equity

**Bootstrapping:**
- Fund with revenue and savings
- Retain full ownership
- Slower growth, more control

**Revenue-Based Financing:**
- Repay as percentage of revenue
- No equity dilution
- Good for recurring revenue businesses

**Grants:**
- Government grants (IRAP, SR&ED)
- Non-dilutive funding
- See grant list in `/ops/funding/`

**Debt:**
- Bank loans
- Business credit
- Personal guarantees often required

**Friends & Family:**
- Raise from personal network
- Simpler terms
- Can strain relationships

## Timeline Expectations

**Typical Seed Fundraising Timeline:**

- **Month 1-2:** Preparation (deck, financials, outreach list)
- **Month 2-3:** Initial outreach and meetings
- **Month 3-4:** First meetings and follow-ups
- **Month 4-5:** Due diligence and term sheets
- **Month 5-6:** Negotiation and closing

**Total: 4-6 months (can be longer)**

## Red Flags to Avoid

- Raising too early (before traction)
- Raising too much (dilution)
- Valuation too high (hard to raise next round)
- Bad co-founders or team issues
- Not knowing your numbers
- Overselling or lying
- Not listening to feedback

## Success Metrics

**Good Signs:**
- Investors ask for follow-up meetings
- Multiple investors interested
- Term sheets received
- Due diligence progressing

**Warning Signs:**
- No follow-up interest
- Constant rejections
- Can't get meetings
- Investors not in your space
